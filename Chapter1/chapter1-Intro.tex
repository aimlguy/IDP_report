\section{Introduction}
Industrial energy management has become an important part of modern manufacturing because energy cost directly affects production cost and profit. Micro, Small and Medium Enterprises often operate with limited resources, older machinery and basic monitoring systems \cite{ref21,ref22,ref23,ref24,ref25,ref26,ref27}. Many MSMEs depend on both grid electricity and diesel generators. Grid electricity is cheaper, but diesel-generator power is expensive and produces higher carbon dioxide emissions \cite{ref28,ref29,ref30,ref31,ref32}.

Traditional energy monitoring systems mainly show present or past energy consumption. They do not predict future energy demand, detect abnormal usage or suggest better operating schedules. This creates a need for intelligent systems that can collect energy data, analyze it and support decision-making.

Industry 4.0 technologies such as the Internet of Things, Artificial Intelligence and Digital Twin technology provide a solution to this problem. IoT can collect real-time energy data from machines and meters. A digital twin can create a virtual model of the industrial energy system. AI and machine learning can predict future consumption, detect abnormal patterns and recommend energy-saving actions.

In this project, an AI-driven digital twin is developed for smart energy optimization in MSMEs. The system uses production and energy data to predict total energy consumption, analyze generator usage, calculate cost and emissions, and simulate different energy-saving scenarios. The aim is to help MSMEs reduce energy wastage, lower operating cost and improve sustainability.

\section{Literature Review}
Digital twin technology has been widely studied for smart manufacturing and industrial energy management. Earlier studies explain how digital twins create virtual models of real systems and help in monitoring, simulation and optimization. Tao et al. discussed the use of digital twins in industry and explained their importance in smart manufacturing. Kritzinger et al. reviewed digital twin applications in manufacturing and described different types of digital twin models.

Research on energy-efficient digital twins shows that virtual models can be used to analyze production behavior and energy consumption under different operating conditions. Studies on IoT-based energy monitoring explain how sensors, smart meters and cloud dashboards can help industries monitor voltage, current, power and energy consumption.

Machine learning has also been used for energy prediction. Regression models, Random Forest, XGBoost and deep learning models such as LSTM can predict future energy demand from historical data. Anomaly detection methods can identify abnormal energy usage caused by machine faults, sudden load increase or inefficient operation.

However, many existing systems focus only on one part of the problem. Some systems only monitor energy, while others only predict demand. Many studies are designed for large industries and may not be suitable for MSMEs. There is a need for a low-cost integrated framework that combines data collection, KPI analysis, prediction, scenario simulation, anomaly detection and dashboard-based decision support.

\subsection{Summary of Literature Gap}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Most digital twin systems are designed for large industries and are not cost-effective for MSMEs.
    \item Many systems provide monitoring but do not include prediction and optimization.
    \item Existing studies often focus on a single technique such as regression, ML prediction or anomaly detection.
    \item Few systems combine energy KPIs, machine learning, generator-usage prediction, scenario simulation and dashboard support in one platform.
    \item MSMEs require a simple, low-cost and interpretable solution for practical energy management.
    \item This project addresses these gaps by developing an integrated AI-driven digital twin framework for MSME energy optimization.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Motivation}
The motivation for this project comes from the need to reduce energy wastage and operating cost in MSMEs. Many small and medium manufacturing units use diesel generators during peak-load periods or grid limitations. Diesel-generator energy is more expensive and produces higher emissions than grid energy. If energy demand can be predicted in advance, the factory can shift loads, reduce generator dependency and avoid unnecessary cost \cite{ref33,ref34,ref35,ref36,ref37,ref38,ref39,ref40}.

A digital twin allows the industry to test operational changes virtually before applying them in the real plant. This reduces risk and helps managers take better decisions. By combining AI, IoT-based data and dashboard visualization, MSMEs can move from simple energy monitoring to intelligent energy management.

\section{Problem Statement}
The increasing energy demand in MSMEs leads to higher costs, inefficient energy usage and higher diesel-generator dependency. Traditional monitoring systems lack advanced analysis, prediction and optimization capability. They do not forecast future load, identify abnormal consumption or simulate the effect of operational changes before implementation.

Therefore, there is a need to develop an AI-driven digital twin system that can analyze production and energy data, predict future energy demand, identify energy-saving opportunities, reduce diesel-generator usage and provide real-time decision support for MSME energy optimization.

\section{Objectives}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item To collect and structure MSME energy data such as production output, grid energy and diesel-generator energy.
    \item To calculate energy performance indicators such as total energy, Energy Per Unit, efficiency, cost, carbon dioxide emissions and generator dependency.
    \item To develop regression-based mathematical models for predicting energy consumption from production output.
    \item To implement machine learning models for energy demand prediction and diesel-generator activation alert.
    \item To develop a digital twin simulation framework for testing what-if scenarios such as DG reduction, production increase and base-load reduction.
    \item To design a dashboard-based decision support system for visualizing KPIs, forecasts, anomalies and load scheduling recommendations.
    \item To support sustainable industrial operation by reducing energy cost and carbon emissions.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Brief Methodology of the Project}
The project methodology starts with collecting historical MSME energy data. The dataset contains daily production values, grid energy consumption and diesel-generator energy consumption. The data is imported into MATLAB and cleaned by removing missing, negative and invalid values.

After preprocessing, KPIs are calculated to understand the energy performance of the system. Linear and quadratic regression models are developed to predict energy consumption based on production. The better model is selected using $R^2$ and RMSE values.

Random Forest, XGBoost and an LSTM network are implemented and BENCHMARKED for energy prediction. The Random Forest initially showed $R^2 = 0.94$ but was discarded due to test leakage (Production was linearly dependent on total energy). XGBoost alerting and LSTM forecasting were also excluded from the final twin due to insufficient data points (only 59 records, 6 DG-active days) making them unreliable. Therefore, Standard Linear and Quadratic Regression are selected as the optimal, mathematically sound predictive core.

A digital twin simulation engine is then developed. It simulates different operating scenarios and calculates their effect on energy, cost and emissions. Finally, a dashboard interface is proposed to display KPIs, trends, predictions, what-if results and anomaly alerts.

\section{Assumptions Made / Constraints of the Project}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item The analysis is based on historical production and energy data.
    \item Grid energy tariff is assumed as Rs. 6 per kWh.
    \item Diesel-generator tariff is assumed as Rs. 15 per kWh.
    \item Diesel-generator CO2 emission factor is taken as $\alpha = 0.8$ kg CO2 per kWh (IPCC). The grid carbon-intensity factor is taken as $\gamma = 0.71$ kg CO2 per kWh, from the Central Electricity Authority (CEA) CO2 Baseline Database. Total CO2 therefore accounts for BOTH grid and generator energy.
    \item The safe grid limit is estimated from the historical maximum grid energy with a safety margin.
    \item The current implementation uses spreadsheet-based data and not live IoT sensor data.
    \item The dataset is limited to a short observation period, so further validation is required for large-scale deployment.
    \item The system does not currently include detailed power-quality parameters such as voltage imbalance, harmonics and power factor.
\end{enumerate}
