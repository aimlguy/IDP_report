\section{Mathematical Equations Used}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Total Energy: $E_{total} = E_{grid} + E_{DG}$
    \item Energy Efficiency: $\eta = \frac{Production}{Total Energy}$
    \item Energy Per Unit: $EPU = \frac{Total Energy}{Production}$
    \item Operating Cost: $Cost = 6 \times E_{grid} + 15 \times E_{DG}$
    \item CO2 Emission: $CO_2 = (0.8 \times E_{DG}) + (0.71 \times E_{Grid})$
    \item Linear Regression: $\hat{E} = mP + c$
    \item Quadratic Regression: $\hat{E} = aP^2 + bP + c$
    \item RMSE: $RMSE = \sqrt{\frac{\sum(E_{actual} - E_{predicted})^2}{n}}$
    \item Safe Grid Limit: $Safe Grid Limit = 0.95 \times Maximum Grid Energy$
\end{enumerate}

\section{Interpretation of Results}
The results show that total energy consumption increases with production. However, energy efficiency also improves at higher production levels because fixed energy consumption is distributed across more units. This means the Energy Per Unit decreases when production increases.

Testing outcomes validate that for the given 59-record dataset, simple quadratic and linear regression reliably establish the baseline relationship between production volume and expected energy use. Complex models (Random Forest) overfitted severely due to multicollinearity and limited data. The standard mathematical model successfully bounds demand, establishing a reliable baseline twin.

Because grid electricity is not carbon-free ($\gamma = 0.71$ kg/kWh), only the difference between the diesel and grid factors is genuinely avoided when load is shifted from the generator to the grid; the avoided-CO2 figures above are reported on this net basis.

The DG reduction scenario shows that reducing generator dependency gives direct financial and environmental benefits. A 50\% reduction in DG usage saves 35,190 MU and avoids 345.3 kg of CO2 on a net basis.

The load optimization scenario shows that shifting 515 kWh of load from DG to grid supply can save 4,635 MU and avoid 45.5 kg of net CO2 on the peak-demand day. This proves that load scheduling can reduce both cost and emissions.

The anomaly detection system helps identify days where energy consumption is unusually high or low. This can support maintenance decisions and help detect inefficient machine operation.

Overall, the results prove that an AI-driven digital twin can help MSMEs move from reactive monitoring to predictive and optimized energy management.
